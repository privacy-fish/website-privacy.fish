privacy.fish is hosted in Norwegian data centers. That matters for both operational trust and legal exposure.

## What the service commits to

The service keeps only the log data that Norwegian law legally requires of email providers. It does not collect names, phone numbers, or other unnecessary personal details simply because they might be useful later.

When legally binding requests arrive, the service position is straightforward:

- comply only with obligations that actually apply,
- disclose the minimum data legally required,
- challenge overbroad requests where legally possible.

## What jurisdiction does not solve

No hosting location makes lawful court orders disappear. A serious threat model should say this plainly.

privacy.fish cannot protect users from lawful Norwegian court orders and other legally binding requests from Norwegian authorities. What it can do is reduce the amount of retained data available to disclose in the first place.

## Why minimization matters more than slogans

Jurisdiction is only one layer of the trust model. The more important layer is data minimization.

If a provider keeps large amounts of user data indefinitely, even a favorable jurisdiction leaves users exposed. If a provider keeps much less data, for less time, the blast radius of legal and technical events is smaller.

That is why privacy.fish emphasizes architecture and operations over branding language. "Privacy" only matters if the system has been constrained to support it.
